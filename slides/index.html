Skill: Electronics
- To tap into this world of circuits and components you’ll need to understand the interactions and relationships between voltage, current and resistance. - There are lots of analogies to existing physical phenomena (water, air, chains/pulleys) but they’re never perfect.
- Giant tome was recommended to me (Practical Electronics for Inventors). Good for deep diving into particular concepts or components. - Youtube videos
- Basic building blocks of any circuit are components, you've heard of resistors, capacitors and transistors - Also will be building using Integrated Circuits (ICs) - - In software you *could* write all the functionality from scratch, but more complex and more likely to get something wrong - Same thing in electronics. Many Integrated Circuits (ICs) exist that perform complex or common tasks in a neat package. - charging a battery cell, converting from one protocol to another or complex sensors. - Microcontroller = IC = mini computer
- Making a LED matrix means we need LEDs, a LED driver and a microcontroller - Power input and probably some passive components - Resistors can reduce the voltage to a component or provide a connection to power or ground resulting in a consistent input to a component. - Capacitors absorb spikes in voltage and provide a easy supply of current when needed by a component
- So many suppliers and specifications and parameters - Figure out your main requirements, then sort by cost/availability - It’s actually quite fun once you get into it
- Picked my parts: 10c microcontroller, fairly expensive usb c and led driver, standard LEDs - This is how they work together - If you're into AWS you might find this one easier - Needed to get familiar with KiCad through youtube videos. Helped with schematic standards
- Could use modules or breakout boards - Would require no soldering or figuring out supporting components - To me it feels similar to a low/no code platform. Promises the features, lacks the flexibility. Projects feel cobbled together and complexity can get unmanageable fast.
Skill: PCB Design
- connect components directly in a much smaller form factor and for much cheaper. - It feels like the missing part of the electronics projects I was building before. And PCBs look COOL.
- How they work: - Components are soldered to pads on the PCB and traces connect between pads
- PCBs can have multiple layers allowing you to have double sided boards or boards with internal layers for more routing, or power and ground connection - To connect different layers, holes are drilled through the board and filled with copper.
- Choose some board shape/dimensions
- Each component on the schematic will have a “footprint” which defines the copper pad markings it needs to be placed on the board. - Place your components but they may need to be rotated or moved depending on how you want to connect them
- Can place components on either side of the board
- Lots of tools to help you position things
- To connect components on different layers use vias
- To provide components with ground and power you can tap into the relevant plane with a via
- Final board design - Run automated checks, not comprehensive but pretty good for basic mistakes. Definitely not as good as type checking and unit tests
- V1 went for symmetry, didn't play nicely with the USBC components - Missing capacitor for the LED driver - Doesn't utilise most space - Could have made it work
- V2 went for vibes, still coulnd't fit the USBC resistors on the same side
- V3 final design (hopefully works perfectly)
- Not producing the board by hand, will be leaving that part to the professionals - Want to utilise multi-layer designs - low volume orders for boards and components are extremely cheap and high quality - Quick turn around
